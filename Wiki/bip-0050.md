# BIP-0050: March 2013 Chain Fork Post-Mortem

## Summary

A block that had a larger number of total transaction inputs than previously seen was mined and broadcasted. Bitcoin 0.8 nodes were able to handle this, but some pre-0.8 Bitcoin nodes rejected it, causing an unexpected fork of the blockchain.

### Zweck

BIP-0050 is a post-mortem document, not a protocol proposal. It records what happened during the accidental blockchain fork of March 11–12, 2013 — one of the most significant network incidents in Bitcoin's history.

Bitcoin 0.8 switched the database backend from BerkeleyDB to LevelDB, which removed a hidden per-block lock limit that BerkeleyDB had. When a large block exceeded BerkeleyDB's internal lock count on pre-0.8 nodes, those nodes rejected the block while 0.8 nodes accepted it. The chain split: 0.8 nodes and pre-0.8 nodes followed different tips. At the peak, the two chains diverged by 24 blocks.

The resolution required a coordinated manual response: developers contacted major mining pools via IRC, asked them to temporarily downgrade to 0.7 and orphan the longer 0.8 chain, and coordinated an exchange halt. The pre-0.8 chain was made the canonical chain, and all 0.8 users were asked to downgrade.

BIP-50 is important as a case study in Bitcoin governance under crisis: decentralised consensus, coordination costs, and the role of large miners in unintentional chain splits.
